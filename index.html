A Practical Course · 12 Modules

Reclaim 5–10 hours a week with AI — without losing quality.

Not productivity theater. Work redesign. A sequenced course that teaches you to question, delete, simplify, accelerate, and only then automate the work eating your week.

By Dr. Jason Cherubini, Sellinger School of Business, Loyola University Maryland

TL;DR

Most people adopt AI and end up with more output, not fewer hours. This course fixes that by redesigning your work first, adding AI second, and installing policies that keep the gains.

12 modules. 4–7 short videos each (3–6 min). A workbook, a walk-away asset, and a Custom GPT coaching session per module. Target: reclaim 5–10 hours/week by Module 12.

Why this course exists

Every knowledge worker I know has a version of the same story: they started using ChatGPT or Claude, got faster at drafts and summaries, and then somehow ended up working more. The tools didn't reclaim their time; the system around them absorbed the gains.

The problem isn't the tools. It's that productivity and redesign are different things. Productivity makes a broken system run faster. Redesign makes you question whether the system should exist at all.

This course is built on a stack of ideas — Ferriss on lifestyle design, Allen on trusted systems, McKeown on essentialism, Newport on deep work, Clear on habits, Eyal on attention, and the engineering algorithm of question → delete → simplify → accelerate → automate — reframed for the AI era.

The Spine

CLEARAI — The course operating framework

CLEARAI is the 7-letter sequence that runs the course. It embeds an older engineering principle — question, delete, simplify, accelerate, automate — and reorders it around what AI makes newly possible. Automation is last, not first.

The order-of-operations inside CLEARAI

Embedded in the framework is an engineering algorithm about how to strip bureaucracy out of anything. The order matters. Most AI efforts fail because people skip to step 5.

1Questionevery requirement
2Deleteany part you can
3Simplifywhat remains
4Acceleratecycle time
5Automatewhat's left
The most common mistake Automating a process that should have been deleted. Optimization and automation are the last two steps — not the first.

What the course borrows, from whom

  • Tim Ferriss — lifestyle design; selective focus; elimination of low-value work.
  • David Allen (GTD) — capture, clarify, organize, reflect, engage. Trusted systems beat memory.
  • Greg McKeown (Essentialism) — disciplined prioritization; the hidden cost of yes.
  • Cal Newport — deep work, digital minimalism, asynchronous norms, slow productivity.
  • James Clear — habits and environment design.
  • Nir Eyal — managing internal and external triggers for distraction.
  • Engineering-style algorithms for stripping bureaucracy out of any process.

Each idea shows up where it's most useful — not as a standalone chapter, but as a tool in the module where it earns its keep.

What you need going in

  • Some recurring cognitive work (writing, synthesis, coordination, analysis, communication).
  • Enough autonomy to change how you work, not just what you produce.
  • Willingness to audit honestly and delete aggressively.
  • Access to one major AI tool — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Microsoft Copilot. Any is fine.

What you don't need

  • Technical skills. This is not a coding course.
  • A specific industry. Examples are intentionally broad.
  • A team to implement with. Solo practitioners get the full value.

Prompt Library

Prompts you can steal right now

Every prompt here uses the RCTCO structure from Module 6: Role, Context, Task, Constraints, Output. Replace the {placeholders} with your specifics and use these on any major AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot).

1. Summarize (source-grounded)

ROLE: Senior analyst helping me process {pilot domain} material.
CONTEXT: I'm building a brief for {audience} on {topic}.
TASK: Summarize the source below using ONLY its content. Flag any inference.
CONSTRAINTS: Plain language, no filler, ≤250 words.
OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Central thesis (1 sentence)
- Key points (3–5 bullets)
- Implications for {audience} (2–3 bullets)
- Open questions or gaps (bullets)
- Uncertainty flags

SOURCE:
{paste here}

2. Draft (in-voice)

ROLE: Ghostwriter matching my voice.
CONTEXT: Audience is {audience}. Purpose: {purpose}. Tone: {tone}.
TASK: Draft a {email/memo/post} based on the brief below.
CONSTRAINTS: ≤{word count}. Match the exemplars provided. Flag anything unclear.
OUTPUT FORMAT: Subject line + body. No preamble.

EXEMPLARS OF MY VOICE:
{paste 2–3 samples}

BRIEF:
{paste here}

3. Compare (matrix)

ROLE: Senior research analyst.
CONTEXT: I have {N} sources on {topic}. I need to see where they agree and disagree.
TASK: Build a comparison matrix.
CONSTRAINTS: Neutral tone. Attribute every claim to its source. Flag gaps.
OUTPUT FORMAT:
Markdown table — rows: sources; columns: thesis, evidence, conclusions, disagreements.
Follow with a 3-bullet synthesis.

SOURCES:
{paste here}

4. Transform (rewrite for audience)

ROLE: Communication strategist.
CONTEXT: I have a draft written for {original audience}. I need it for {new audience}.
TASK: Rewrite keeping the facts, changing the register, vocabulary, and length to fit.
CONSTRAINTS: No new claims. Flag anything that requires a new source for the new audience.
OUTPUT FORMAT: Rewritten version, then a short note on what I changed and why.

DRAFT:
{paste here}

5. Extract (action items + decisions)

ROLE: Meeting scribe.
CONTEXT: Below is a transcript/notes from {meeting name}. Attendees: {names}.
TASK: Produce the full follow-up package.
CONSTRAINTS: No filler. Every action item has an owner and deadline.
OUTPUT FORMAT:
1. Summary (≤150 words)
2. Decisions (bullets)
3. Action items (table: Owner | Action | Due)
4. Open questions
5. Draft follow-up email to attendees

TRANSCRIPT:
{paste here}
The shortcut Name and save these prompts in your operating system's Templates folder. The anchor-prompts library is a one-time setup that pays for itself within a week.

The AI Lifestyle Design Coach

One GPT. Twelve modes. A coach, not a tutor.

What it is

A single custom GPT configured with module-specific knowledge, asset templates, and a strict operating style. Every session ends with a completed artifact and a concrete next action within 48 hours.

Operating rules (excerpt from the system prompt)

  1. Start every session by asking which module the student is working on.
  2. Ask one question at a time when the student seems uncertain or overloaded.
  3. Never deliver generic advice. Every response ties to producing the module's walk-away asset.
  4. Never let the student leave a session without a completed artifact.
  5. Default to structured output: tables, matrices, checklists.
  6. Challenge weak assumptions. If the student scores everything "high value," push back.
  7. Always end with a concrete next action within 48 hours.
  8. Automation is taught as last — never first.
  9. Flag regulated content (FERPA, HIPAA, client-confidential, legal privilege) explicitly.

About & Contact

Who made this

Dr. Jason Cherubini, Sellinger School of Business, Loyola University Maryland. Part of a small series of practical AI courses including AI for Students and AI for Faculty.

Why it exists

Most AI content teaches tools. This course teaches a system. Tools change every quarter; a system for redesigning work around AI is durable.

What it is, and isn't

  • Is: a sequenced, practical 12-module course for knowledge workers with recurring cognitive work.
  • Is: opinionated about order — diagnose, eliminate, build, augment, redesign, armor, institutionalize.
  • Isn't: a promise of a literal four-hour week.
  • Isn't: a tool shootout. Tools are mentioned only where they change what's possible.
